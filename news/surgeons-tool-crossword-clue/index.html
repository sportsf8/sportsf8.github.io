Today’s Theme

The four grayed patches of squares in Ms. Chung and Mr. Ni’s grid represent rolling DICE in a progressive pattern as we move from top to bottom.

Image Credit… The New York Times

What usually messes solvers up at this point is what that particular theme element does to the longer answers. The thing is, the element is consistent throughout the theme, so once you get one, you should be able to get them all. The rolling DICE may change the order of the letters in the gray squares, but the way the theme works stays the same. Hang in there, it will come to you.

So what’s happening here? Well, Ms. Chung and Mr. Ni offer us a set of four fairly common phrases that need the letters DICE to complete them. For example, at the central entry, 36A, the answer to the clue “Reference that arranges words by concept rather than alphabetically” is REVERSE DICTIONARY. But when you fill in the entry that stretches across the grid, your answer will be REVERSECTIONARY, which almost sounds as if it could be a surgical procedure, but it’s not.

We need to jump up a line to capture the DI in DICE that are the last two letters of AUDI at 34A. Think of the entry at 36A as an inchworm, and the EDIC in the gray square is where it hunches up to move forward.

“‘Do I Not Destroy My Enemies When I Make Them My Friends?’”

TUESDAY PUZZLE — I had two reactions to this puzzle by Ross Trudeau. The first was, “This is fun, and that’s definitely a hat in the grid,” and the second one was, “Dude! Where’s Mr. Peanut? Or Rich Uncle Pennybags, that guy from Monopoly?”

This is an important point. Sometimes you can enjoy a puzzle so much that you just want there to be … more. There are quite a few examples of people and characters who would qualify to be part of this theme set based on the revealer at 45D, and Mr. Trudeau managed to fit four good ones in. I just couldn’t help but feel as if he could have tucked at least one more really familiar one in with a slightly different grid design.

Either way, this is a nice, smooth Tuesday puzzle, and I doff my 45D to Mr. Trudeau.

Today’s Theme

Mr. White offers us a set of five common “X in the Y” idioms, such as “Bird in the hand?” and some of the squares in each theme entry contain circles. Those circles, when read from left to right, spell out a type of “X,” and the entire theme entry is a synonym for Y, so that the “X” is in the “Y.”

For example, the answer to 17A’s “Bird in the hand?” is HIRED PERSON. That makes very little sense, but take a closer look at the circles: They spell “HERON.” A HERON is a type of bird, and HIRED PERSON is a synonym for “hand,” as in a ranch hand.

Similarly, at 24A, the answer to the clue “Snake in the grass?” is CATTLE FODDER. The circled letters spell ADDER, a type of snake. “Grass” is CATTLE FODDER.
